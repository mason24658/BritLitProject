Struggles Between the Sacred and the Profane in Early British Literature

Exploring key works and their reflections on the tension between devotion and worldly desires.

Introduction

The theme of "Struggles Between the Sacred and the Profane" pervades many texts in early British literature. This tension is especially visible in works like Doctor Faustus by Christopher Marlowe and The Book of Margery Kempe by Margery Kempe. These texts not only reflect the internal conflict faced by individuals, but also demonstrate the societal challenges and spiritual battles between faith and worldly temptations.

Doctor Faustus (Class Text)

Summary: In Marlowe's Doctor Faustus, Faustus seeks knowledge and power at the cost of his soul. His pact with the devil places him at the crossroads of sacred salvation and the profane pursuit of self-gratification. The play explores the consequences of rejecting divine grace for the sake of worldly and intellectual ambition.

Analysis: Faustus' journey exemplifies the ultimate tragedy of human pride and disillusionment with the sacred. Despite repeated opportunities for redemption, he refuses to repent, reflecting the dangers of succumbing to the lure of the profane at the expense of spiritual well-being.

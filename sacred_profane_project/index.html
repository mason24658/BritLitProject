Struggles Between the Sacred and the Profane in Early British Literature

Introduction

The theme of the sacred versus the profane is a recurring element in early British literature, reflecting the tension between religious devotion and secular desires. This tension is embodied in various characters and narratives, illustrating the complexities of faith, sin, and redemption.

The Tragic History of Doctor Faustus by Christopher Marlowe

In Marlowe’s play, Doctor Faustus turns away from God in his pursuit of forbidden knowledge and worldly power, ultimately falling victim to his own desires. The play dramatizes the struggle between the sacred (spiritual salvation) and the profane (worldly ambition), with Faustus’s tragic downfall as the culmination of his choices.

The Book of Margery Kempe by Margery Kempe

Margery Kempe’s life story provides a different portrayal of the sacred and the profane. Her intense religious devotion often puts her at odds with her family, community, and the church, highlighting the conflict between the sacred and the profane in her personal life.

Comparison of the Texts

Both texts illustrate the theme of the struggle between sacred and profane, though from different perspectives. Faustus’s journey represents the dangerous allure of secular knowledge and power, while Kempe’s life reflects the challenges of living a devout life in a corrupt world.
